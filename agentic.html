01 [ 01 ] // the pattern

Same shape,
every time.

Every build follows the same shape. The client gets their own VPS, usually a small Hetzner box that costs less than a pizza a month. On it runs an agent runtime, OpenClaw or Hermes depending on the job, wired to connectors for whatever the work touches: a mailbox, a CRM, a task tracker, an ad account.

The AI itself is bring-your-own-key. The client creates their own Claude account and the system runs on their API key. Their data goes through their account, billed to them, visible to them. I never sit in the middle of it, and there is no SaaS subscription to me or anyone else.

The interface is whatever chat app the team already lives in. No new dashboard, no login to forget. You message the bot the way you'd message a colleague.

02 [ 02 ] // furtados

Twelve stores,
one email.

Furtados runs 12 stores across Mumbai. Every store emails a daily accounting report to one central mailbox, and until recently somebody had to open all 12, cross-check the figures by eye and decide what needed attention before close of day. Thirty to sixty minutes of copy-paste, every single evening.

Now a bot on their own server wakes up every evening, pulls the day's reports over IMAP, parses whatever format each store happened to send (Excel, CSV, PDF, the formats drift), consolidates everything into one master sheet and checks each line against rules the team can edit in a plain config file. No code changes needed to tune a threshold.

  • Cash in drawer doesn't match the POS total? CRITICAL.
  • A store hasn't submitted by cutoff? An early alert fires so someone can chase them before the nightly run.
  • Revenue far below that store's own 7-day average, with no holiday on record? Flagged.
  • Refund ratio creeping up, an expense line jumping, totals that don't add up? All flagged, each with its own severity.

One email lands in the manager's inbox. The subject line shows the flag count at a glance, flags are pasted inline, the master sheet is attached, and a recap section tracks whether yesterday's criticals were actually resolved or just ignored. The bot runs in under two minutes and exits.

03 [ 03 ] // pocket fund

A chief of staff
that lives in Slack.

Pocket Fund is a micro private equity firm I work with, and their setup has grown into a small crew of agents, each with exactly one job.

The chief-of-staff pair, Cyndra and Nanoclaw, sit in Slack. Meeting transcripts get dropped into a channel and the agents classify them, pull out decisions and action items, and suggest tasks. Nothing enters the task tracker without a human tapping approve. Once approved, the task gets filed, assigned and chased.

The content agents handle the newsletter and the blog. They research with Google Trends and keyword data, suggest topics people are actually searching for, draft the articles and push them to Beehiiv for review. Nobody publishes anything a human hasn't read.

The ads agent builds Meta campaigns. Someone sends the creative over Telegram, a video or a few images, tells the bot the budget and audience, and the campaign gets set up from chat.

04 [ 04 ] // kautilya

An org chart
made of agents.

Kautilya runs an ambassador programme for chartered accountants, which means outreach to a database of thousands of CAs across India. Too many for a person, too nuanced for a mail-merge.

The system is run by an orchestrator agent named Chanakya. Chank, to the team. Chanakya doesn't send anything itself. It delegates: a strategist agent decides who to contact and when, writers draft the emails, senders dispatch them in controlled batches, and a tracker watches what happens next. Opens and clicks land in a Convex database. When someone replies with genuine interest, the system reads the thread and drafts a response for a human to send.

It is a management hierarchy, except the middle managers cost a few rupees a day and never forget to follow up.

05 [ 05 ] // smaller builds

Personal bots
& odd jobs.

Not everything is a company system. Jade is a research-and-outreach agent in the works for store events: it finds artists and authors worth contacting, builds the shortlist and then works LinkedIn and email. I've also built personal assistant bots that run calendars and notes over Telegram, and SEO bots that watch keyword movements and turn them into content briefs.

Most of these start the same way: someone describes a chore they're sick of, and a couple of weeks later there's a bot quietly doing it.

runtimeOpenClaw / Hermes on an Ubuntu VPS. Cron for schedules, SQLite for audit logs. One box per client.
memoryMarkdown context packs describing the team, preferences and rules. The agent reads them on every session and treats them as ground truth. When reality disagrees, it flags the difference instead of silently overwriting.
surfacesTelegram, WhatsApp, Slack and plain email. Meet people where they already are.
guardrailsBring-your-own-key, so data stays on the client's account. And a human approves anything that leaves the building: emails, posts, tasks, campaigns.
